Dec 20, 2020

My project is focused on supporting traditionally underrepresented students in computer science. This past summer, I collaborated with a team of other Rindge students in designing and teaching a 2-week virtual summer APCS/APCSP prep course associated with the CRLS MPP program. Since then, I have been working under the mentorship of my advisor and Ms. Fitzgerald in understanding theories and essential literature in the topic of education for liberation and including those ideas into my project. During my midterm presentation, for instance, I talked about incorporating wisdom from Ella Baker and Paulo Freire into the next phase of our project, which entails working with our current cohort of students in redesigning the summer CS curriculum. Recently, I have been working with other CRLS students in designing a school-wide equity-focused hackathon; once we figure out the logistics of this one-week virtual event, we would invite our cohort of summer students to volunteer in debugging / Q&A sessions during the hackathon. As a high school senior, this project matters a lot to me. Throughout these four years of high school, I have studied computer science in various settings, including taking CS classes at Rindge, self-studying, attending 24-hour hackathon events, and programming as part of internships and projects in the States, France, and Taiwan. Having had these experiences, I understand the importance of amplifying opportunities and providing support systems for new programmers from underrepresented groups in computing. With this project, my team and I hope to be able to encourage students of more backgrounds at Rindge to try CS.

In terms of launching ambitious social innovation initiatives, from my experiences, I think that it is important to understand the “field” in which your project would be situated. Regarding my initiative focused on Equity in Computing, I spent the first few months looking into different types of sources regarding existing opinions and case studies. For instance, I talked to different students and teachers at Rindge; I also read about relative initiatives from around the world. Personally, when launching new projects, I think it is worth dedicating at least two months to just understanding the field and potential problems to solve. Afterwards, when iterating through different ideas, teams should still run their ideas through different people; this is similar to how many startups choose to conduct focus groups. Ultimately, I think social innovation initiatives rely on iteratively building new knowledge of the unique set of problems they work to solve.

Summary of Roadblocks I encountered along the way.

  • Around late September, I wasn't really sure where and how to navigate familiarizing myself with sources related to educatio for liberation. I resolved this by talking to my advisor, Mr. Montero, asking him for his advice. Ultimately, he helped me craft a reading list.
  • After my research phase, I encountered another roadblock: this time, I was deciding between writing a senior thesis culminating my experiences ad continuing to read about initiatives and developing more. In the end, after talking to advisors, I decided to continue to work o developing initiatives and saving the senior thesis idea for another time.

  • The last phase of this project (planning the hackathon) was completely unexpected, although planning a hackathon has been a dream since attending one during my freshman year.

Aspirations that arose while working on this project.

  • Before working on this project, I didn't know that I would find education studies to be one of the favorite topics I have studied so far. After spending so may months on my project, I am looking forward to take education related social science courses at college, and even serving as a research assistant for professors.
  • I regained my interest for project/product development/management while working on my Senior Project. Although I spent a lot of time working on personal projects similar to this during my sophomore year, while in France, I forgot about them once I started my junior year in Massachusetts.
